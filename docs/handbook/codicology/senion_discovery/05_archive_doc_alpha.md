### Derivational suffix shapes you may meet in this document

The Pre-Threshold Tongue forms many words by appending a small set of derivational suffixes to a root. Recognising the suffix tells you *what kind of thing* the word is, even when you do not know the root.

| Suffix | What it makes a word mean |
|---|---|
| **-er** | a *person* or *agent* who does the root: a *do-er*. |
| **-ul** | a *place* or *room* associated with the root. |
| **-ith** | a *tool* or *instrument* for the root. |
| **-om** | a *document* or *form* associated with the root. |
| **-il** | an *adjective* meaning *related to* the root. |
| **-veth** | a *state* or *condition* associated with the root. |

So **arveler** is *an archive person* (an archivist). **Arvelul** is *the archive room*. **Selveth** would be *the state of silence*. The suffixes are not new vocabulary so much as predictable productivity — you can derive them yourself for any root in your slip.

### Note on `-mor` (line 3)

You saw `-mor` on the stone (*sen-mor*: six witnesses). It is a **numeral classifier** used when the things counted are *people*. **`-tor`** (line 3 also) is a **postposition** meaning *beside*; it is in the same family as the postpositions you already know.
