## A. Tense markers

The verb cluster has two tense suffixes you need. They attach to the verb root **before** the aspect marker (if any), the mood marker (if any), and the evidential.

| English | Senion |
|---|---|
| 1. The door opens. I see it. (already known) | **Kor morh-or-nai.** |
| 2. The door **opened**. I saw it. | **Kor morh-us◇-ren◇-nai.** |
| 3. The door **will open**. The record says so. | **Kor morh-thel◇-seln.** |
| 4. The witness read the record. I saw it. | **Osher kelhom thuri-us-ren-nai.** |
| 5. The signal **will come** from below. The record says so. | **Sūn nēr-aru nadi-thel-seln.** |

**Working-group note.** Two markers carry the tense:

- **`-ren`** — past. Attached after the verb root (and after any aspect marker).
- **`-thel`** — future. Same position.
- (No suffix) — present / habitual.

The aspect marker **`-us`** marks a **completed** action — sometimes glossed as English *has done*, *did*, or simply the past with a flavour of finality. You will see `-us-ren` (completed-and-past) often in archival sentences; the stone uses it once, in line II: *the first door **has opened**.* You may now read that line.

## B. Sealed mood `-vor`

The mood particle **`-vor`** marks the clause as **sealed**. A sealed clause is one whose unauthorised transcription, repetition, or recall is restricted under the speaker's clearance ring. It attaches to the verb after tense and **before the evidential**.

| English | Senion |
|---|---|
| 6. The door is open. The record says so. (already known) | **Kor han-seln.** |
| 7. The door is open. **[sealed.]** The record says so. | **Kor han-vor-seln.** |
| 8. The first door opened. **[sealed.]** I saw it. | **Kor en-eth morh-us-ren-vor-nai.** |

**Working-group note.** `-vor` does not make a statement *secret* in the modern sense. It marks the statement as belonging to a class of utterances whose repetition is restricted by the Foundation's clearance schema. A sealed claim **can be true and a sealed claim can be false** — the seal is a procedural status, not an evidential one.

Staff who confuse the two are addressed via Form D-17(b).

## C. Numerals (base six) and the classifier `-mor`

Senion counts in **base six**. The first six numerals are:

| Decimal | Senion |
|---|---|
| 1 | **en**◇ |
| 2 | **dū**◇ |
| 3 | **thren**◇ |
| 4 | **korv**◇ |
| 5 | **pem**◇ |
| 6 | **sen** |

(You will recognise **sen** from the stone: *six witnesses read the record*.)

A numeral that counts **people** takes the classifier **`-mor`◇** between the numeral and the noun (or, in the form on the stone, attached to the numeral as a suffix). A numeral that counts **objects** uses a different classifier you will meet later.

| English | Senion |
|---|---|
| 9. Three witnesses read the record. The record says so. | **Osher thren-mor kelhom thuri-or-seln.** |
| 10. The first door is in the archive. I saw it. | **Kor en-eth arvel-eth han-nai.** |

In line 10, **`en-eth`** is the noun phrase *first* (*one-at*); Senion uses postpositional case to mark ordinals. There is no separate ordinal series.

## D. Free recall

Translate yourself, then re-check after Memo γ.

| English | (your translation) |
|---|---|
| 11. The witness read the record yesterday. I saw it. | |
| 12. The door will open. **[sealed.]** The record says so. | |
| 13. Five witnesses are in the archive. I infer it. | |
| 14. The name was at the threshold. The record says so. | |

You do not need a Senion word for *yesterday* to render line 11. Senion expresses *past* by suffix, not by a separate adverb. *Yesterday* and *previously* and *long ago* are all spoken in the same morpheme.

This is one of the points where Stillwater staff begin to overinterpret the language. Try not to.
