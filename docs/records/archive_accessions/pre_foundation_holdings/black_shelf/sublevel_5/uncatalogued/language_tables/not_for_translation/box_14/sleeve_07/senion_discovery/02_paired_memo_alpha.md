# Paired Memo α — Reading-Room Internal

**Subject:** First-pass evidential drill (bilingual).
**From:** Codicology working group / Office of Inherited Silences
**To:** Reading-room staff currently working on the Roseta-stone packet
**Form:** SW-COD-MEMO-α

This memo is **bilingual on purpose**. The English column states what the working group meant. The Senion column is how a staff member trained in the Pre-Threshold Tongue would render it. The two are aligned line for line; an unaligned line is an error and should be reported via Form C-7.

Words you should already recognise from the stone are marked with † on first use. Words you have **not** seen yet are marked with ◇. You will only meet a handful of ◇ words in this memo.

## Lines 1 — 4 (the four core evidentials)

| English | Senion |
|---|---|
| 1. The door† exists.† I saw it. | **Kor han-nai.** |
| 2. The door exists. Someone told me. | **Kor han-kel.** |
| 3. The door exists. I infer it. | **Kor han-thur.**◇ |
| 4. The door exists. The record says so. | **Kor han-seln.†** |

**Working-group note.** The four evidentials above are the **core four**. There are seven in total in the natural language; the remaining three are introduced in Memo γ. For now, fix the four core in memory:

- **`-nai`** — directly witnessed (seen).
- **`-kel`** — reported / heard from another.
- **`-thur`** — inferred from circumstance.
- **`-seln`** — from a written or recorded source.

A declarative sentence in Senion **cannot omit** the evidential. To omit it is treated as evasion by the Codicology working group, not as ordinary error.

## Lines 5 — 8 (postpositions on familiar nouns)

| English | Senion |
|---|---|
| 5. The door is in the archive.† I saw it. | **Kor arvel-eth†‡ han-nai.** |
| 6. The signal came from below. I was told. | **Sūn† nēr-aru† nadi-or-kel.** |
| 7. The name is at the threshold. I saw it. | **Meren† kor-eth dom-or-nai.** |
| 8. The signal came toward me. The record says so. | **Sūn ya-toh◇ nadi-or-seln.** |

‡ The postposition **`-eth`** can be glossed as English *in*, *at*, or *inside* depending on context. The Pre-Threshold Tongue does not distinguish these the way English does.

**Working-group note.** Senion places its *prepositional* words **after** the noun, suffixed with a hyphen. The postpositions you have now seen are:

- **`-eth`** — in / at / inside.
- **`-aru`** — from / out of.
- **`-toh`** — to / toward.

The bound nature of the postposition is conventionally marked with a hyphen in romanisation. In the original script the boundary is not marked at all; staff who reach **Memo γ** without internalising the boundary tend to retreat.

## Lines 9 — 11 (the verb cluster, basic)

| English | Senion |
|---|---|
| 9. The door opens. I see it. | **Kor morh†-or◇-nai.** |
| 10. The witness reads the record. I see them do it. | **Osher† kelhom† thuri†-or-nai.** |
| 11. The archive holds the name. The record says so. | **Arvel meren dom-or-seln.** |

**Working-group note.** Senion verbs are bare in their citation form. The **`-or`** suffix marks an action that is **going on now** or **happens regularly** (the working group calls this *imperfective aspect*; the historical scribes used the same term). A verb without `-or` is either a single completed instance or a state — the distinction is usually clear from context.

You have now seen the verb cluster:

```
verb-root  ( -aspect )  ( -tense )  ( -mood )  -evidential
```

The evidential is the only piece that is mandatory in every declarative clause. The others appear as needed.
